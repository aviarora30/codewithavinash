Manufactured Friction: Learning with AI

Reflections on AI-driven learning, mistakes, and intentional exploration
3 Feb 2026 · #AI #learning #personalGrowth #productivity

I recently started learning n8n and agentic AI systems, and it highlighted how rigid most conventional learning formats are.

Most learning channels I’ve used over the years have a fixed shape: articles, documentation, videos. Even instructor-led sessions, while interactive, usually follow a predefined flow. You can ask questions, but only within the boundaries of the material and the time available.

That structure works well early in a career. Over time, especially as experience grows, it starts to feel restrictive. Real learning becomes less linear, but the formats don’t adapt.

Using AI as the primary learning tool changed that.

What changed with AI wasn’t just speed. It was the shape of learning itself.

Instead of consuming a predefined course or resource, learning started to feel like an ongoing conversation. One that adapts to my experience level and responds to where I’m stuck, rather than forcing me to follow a fixed sequence.

I could branch in any direction without planning for it in advance. Ask a clarification question midway, zoom out to understand the broader context, or slow things down and take one step at a time. The path wasn’t linear anymore, and that felt closer to how I actually think when learning something new.

In that sense, it feels like a tailor-made course, rewritten in real time, just for how I think.

What AI is genuinely good at for learning

The biggest benefit isn’t speed. It’s staying with a problem without breaking momentum.

Earlier, getting unstuck meant context switching: searching articles, scanning documentation, skimming videos, and mentally stitching things together. Often, by the time I found the answer, I had already lost the thread of what I was learning.

With AI, I can stay inside the problem. I can ask a question at the exact point of confusion and get an explanation that fits the context I’m already in.

This has been most useful for:

  • Getting unstuck when something doesn’t work and the reason isn’t obvious
  • Understanding new concepts by relating them to things I already know
  • Exploring adjacent ideas without committing to a full deep dive
  • Controlling pace, moving fast when things are clear and slowing down when they’re not

The value here isn’t completeness. It’s continuity. Learning feels less like jumping between resources and more like extending the same line of thought until it makes sense.

The uncomfortable trade-off

One thing I didn’t expect was how few mistakes I started making while learning.

AI reduces friction so effectively that many small failures simply don’t happen. When something goes wrong, it’s easy to ask for an explanation or a fix and move on. That sounds like a win, and in many ways it is. But some of the most durable lessons I’ve learned over the years didn’t come from answers. They came from mistakes that taught me things I didn’t even know I needed yet.

Those lessons often showed up as a broken assumption, an unexpected constraint, or a side effect that only surfaced because I took a wrong turn.

When learning was slower and more manual, these lessons appeared accidentally. I’d run into a problem, fix it, and come away with an understanding that paid off much later in a different context.

With AI, learning becomes highly intentional. You mostly learn what you ask for. That makes learning focused and efficient, but also narrower. Intentional exploration is harder than accidental discovery, and I have to consciously create space for it now.

What changed in my learning style

AI makes learning smoother and more controlled. To balance that, I now introduce friction intentionally.

When something feels too easy or resolves too quickly, I slow down on purpose. I spend extra time understanding why something works, or try to do parts of it without asking for help, just to see where I struggle.

Earlier, friction appeared naturally through mistakes and dead ends. Now, I have to manufacture it. Depth no longer emerges accidentally; it requires conscious effort.

For mid-senior engineers and managers

For experienced learners, the risk isn’t misinformation. It’s premature clarity.

When answers come too easily, it’s tempting to move on before the idea has fully settled. Over time, that can quietly flatten intuition. You still solve problems and ship outcomes, but you accumulate fewer of the small, hard-earned heuristics that come from sitting with confusion and making wrong turns. That’s a subtle cost, but it matters for long-term craftsmanship.

A few principles I’m trying to keep in mind:

  • Don’t outsource confusion too early. Sitting with uncertainty often reveals better questions.
  • Use AI to explore, not to bypass thinking. If something feels unclear, ask why before asking how.
  • Slow down deliberately when things feel obvious. Obvious is often where assumptions hide.
  • Occasionally struggle through a problem without asking. The friction is part of the learning.

None of this is about rejecting AI. It’s about using it in a way that respects how expertise actually forms over time.

Open questions

I’m still unsure where this leads in the long run.

If learning becomes increasingly intentional and guided, what happens to the kind of intuition that forms through accidents and detours? Does it matter, or does new intuition form in different ways?

Will we get better at manufacturing the right kind of friction, or will convenience slowly win out? And if it does, what do we lose that’s hard to measure today?

I don’t have answers yet. For now, I’m treating AI as a powerful learning companion, not a replacement for struggle. I’m paying attention to what it accelerates, and what it quietly removes.

That feels like a good place to pause.
